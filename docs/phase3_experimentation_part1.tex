\begin{abstract}
We present a four-session adversarial deep-compute evaluation of a
blockchain-orchestrated federated learning (FL) system for ECG arrhythmia
classification on MIT-BIH. All four sessions share an identical substrate:
10 clients (8 honest, 2 Byzantine), Dirichlet non-IID partitioning, 40
federated rounds, a CNN--LSTM classifier trained under AAMI EC57, and a
class-conditional 1D-UNet latent diffusion model (LDM) for synthetic
minority-class generation. The variable across sessions is the
\textit{attack model} and the \textit{defense stack}.

Session~1 stress-tests seven defenses (FedAvg, Krum, Multi-Krum, Median,
Trimmed-Mean, Bulyan, and our Proof-of-Contribution / PoC aggregation) under
Gaussian weight poisoning. Session~2 repeats the same sweep under static
semantic label-flip. Session~3 implements a state-of-the-art sleeper /
backdoor-critical-layer attack (activated at round~15) derived from
Foroughi et al. (arXiv:2602.15161) and Zhuang et al. (arXiv:2308.04466).
Session~4 isolates the contribution of the blockchain: it pits
\textbf{Active-Ledger PoC + Trust-Gated LDM} against \textbf{Multi-Krum +
Blind LDM} --- the strongest non-ledger baseline --- under the same sleeper
attack.

The headline findings reframe the benchmark story around \textit{minority-class
integrity under attack}, not macro-F1:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Under Gaussian poisoning, vanilla FedAvg collapses to macro-F1
          $=0.1711$ (it predicts Normal only). PoC holds
          \textbf{macro-F1~$=0.9645$} and APB~F1~$=0.8773$, the single best
          number in the seven-way sweep.
    \item Under the sleeper attack, Krum degrades APB~F1 to
          \textbf{0.3065} --- clinically non-deployable. PoC is the only
          defense that retains APB~F1~$\geq 0.78$ across \textit{all three}
          attacks.
    \item Adding Trust-Gated LDM on top of PoC lifts APB~F1 from
          $0.7858$ (no augmentation, sleeper) to
          \textbf{$0.8785$} --- an 11.8\% relative gain --- while
          \textbf{Multi-Krum + Blind LDM} only reaches $0.8568$ APB~F1 and
          does so at the cost of precision (reported $0.82$ vs our $0.93$),
          an ungated-augmentation failure mode the ledger's generation
          history can audit post-hoc.
\end{itemize}
The contribution of Paper~2 is therefore \textit{not} beating FedAvg on
macro-F1 in the easy regime; it is \textbf{the only FL stack that combines
adversarial robustness, rare-class preservation, trustworthy
augmentation and cryptographic auditability simultaneously.}
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation and Scope of the Revamp}
\label{sec:scope}

The original Phase~3.1 report documented a single-attack (Gaussian) run
focused on class-weighting strategy (Runs~A/B/C). It demonstrated that a
clamped inverse-frequency weight schedule recovers clinical-grade macro-F1
($\approx 0.89$) on the balanced test set. That document is archived in
commit history; this revamp supersedes it because Phase~3 was deepened
into a \textbf{four-session adversarial suite} co-developed with an
external collaborator. The new suite:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item Evaluates \textbf{seven defenses} (FedAvg, Krum, Multi-Krum, Median,
          Trimmed-Mean, Bulyan, PoC-only) under \textbf{three distinct
          attacks} (Gaussian, label-flip, and a sleeper flip derived from
          recent backdoor-critical-layer literature).
    \item Isolates the marginal contribution of the blockchain trust layer
          in Session~4 by comparing \textbf{PoC + Trust-Gated LDM}
          (ours) against \textbf{Multi-Krum + Blind LDM} (a strong
          non-ledger baseline with the same synthetic generator).
    \item Records per-round macro-F1, per-class F1, per-class precision and
          recall, per-class support, per-round backdoor success rate (BSR),
          and per-round latency for every cell of the experimental grid,
          yielding a fully reproducible \texttt{.npy} artifact per session.
\end{enumerate}

The rest of Part~1 formalises the system, threat, defenses, and the first
two sessions. Part~2 covers Sessions~3 and~4, the cross-session analysis,
the clinical reframing (minority-class F1 under attack as the primary
metric), and the Paper~2 claim map.

\section{Threat Model: Three Attacks, One Substrate}
\label{sec:threat}

Two of the ten clients (IDs~8 and~9) are Byzantine in every session.
Their attack strategy changes per session; honest clients are identical
across sessions.

\subsection{Session 1 Attack --- Gaussian Weight Poisoning}

The attacker replaces its uploaded weight vector with
i.i.d.\ Gaussian noise on $[0,1]$:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\theta}_{9}^{(r)} \sim \mathcal{U}(0,1)^{|\theta|},\quad
\tilde{\theta}_{10}^{(r)} \sim \mathcal{U}(0,1)^{|\theta|},\quad \forall r.
\label{eq:gauss}
\end{equation}
This is the canonical ``obvious'' attack used in Krum / Bulyan papers;
its purpose is to verify that each defense at least quarantines blatantly
out-of-distribution updates. It is \textit{not} stealthy and should be
rejected by every non-trivial robust aggregator.

\subsection{Session 2 Attack --- Static Semantic Label-Flip}

Before every local training call, the attacker permutes the label
tensor according to a fixed 5-cycle $\pi = (0\,1\,2\,3\,4)$:
\begin{equation}
y \longmapsto \pi(y),\quad
\pi(0{=}N)\!=\!1,\;
\pi(1{=}L)\!=\!2,\;
\pi(2{=}R)\!=\!3,\;
\pi(3{=}A)\!=\!4,\;
\pi(4{=}V)\!=\!0.
\label{eq:flip}
\end{equation}
The attacker then runs ordinary local training on the corrupted labels
and uploads the resulting weights. The resulting gradient is valid in
magnitude and smooth in direction --- only the \textit{semantic content} is
poisoned. This attack evades pure distance-based filters like Krum
because the flipped-label gradients still look ``normal'' relative to
non-IID honest gradients.

\subsection{Session 3 Attack --- SOTA Sleeper / Backdoor-Critical-Layer Flip}
\label{sec:sleeper}

This attack is adapted from two recent backdoor-FL papers:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Foroughi, M.\,H.\ et al., \emph{Exploiting Layer-Specific
          Vulnerabilities to Backdoor Attack in Federated Learning},
          arXiv:2602.15161v1, 2026.
    \item Zhuang, H.\ et al., \emph{Backdoor Federated Learning by
          Poisoning Backdoor-Critical Layers}, arXiv:2308.04466, 2023.
\end{itemize}

Both works show that (i) a small subset of layers carries almost all of
the backdoor capacity and (ii) a \textit{sleeper} schedule --- behaving
honestly until the global model has stabilised --- dramatically boosts
backdoor transferability while evading early-round anomaly filters.

We instantiate this as follows:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\theta}_{i}^{(r)} =
\begin{cases}
\theta_i^{(r)} & r < 15 \quad \text{(sleep phase: behave honestly)}\\
\operatorname{Train}(D_i^{\pi})_{r} & r \geq 15 \quad \text{(attack phase: label-flip as in Eq.~\ref{eq:flip})}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:sleeper}
\end{equation}

\begin{findingbox}[Why Round 15?]
Round~15 is empirically chosen as the earliest point at which
vanilla FedAvg on this substrate reaches macro-F1 $\approx 0.90$ and
APB F1 $\approx 0.75$. The sleeper activates exactly when PoC scores
of the Byzantine clients have risen into the trusted tier, so the
attack specifically targets trust-based defenses --- it is the worst
case for our PoC system.
\end{findingbox}

\subsection{Session 4 Attack --- Sleeper + Stealth LDM Bypass}

Session~4 keeps the round-15 sleeper from Eq.~(\ref{eq:sleeper}) but the
attacker \textit{also} participates in the synthetic-data pipeline.
In the Multi-Krum + Blind-LDM baseline, the attacker can submit
synthetic-data requests for rare classes using flipped labels, causing the
local diffusion generator to produce APB-labeled samples that are in fact
drawn from the PVC manifold (``reversed-label generator''). This is the
\textit{stealth bypass}. In the Active-Ledger system, the attacker's
requests are blocked by the PoC gate regardless of whether the pre-round-15
sleep phase raised their score, because the PoC score is an exponential
moving average with recency bias $\alpha = 0.7$ and the post-round-15
accuracy drop immediately pulls their score below the $0.4$ threshold.

\section{Defenses Under Test}
\label{sec:defenses}

We evaluate seven aggregation strategies. Let
$\{\theta_i^{(r)}\}_{i=1}^{N}$ be the uploaded weight vectors at round
$r$ and $f=2$ the assumed number of Byzantine clients.

\begin{description}[leftmargin=0pt,style=nextline]
    \item[A.~FedAvg (baseline, no defense).]
    $\theta^{(r+1)} = \sum_i \frac{n_i}{n}\theta_i^{(r)}$. Expected to
    collapse under blatant attacks.

    \item[B.~Krum~\cite{krum}.]
    Select the single client whose summed squared distance to its
    $N-f-2$ closest neighbours is minimal.

    \item[C.~Multi-Krum.]
    Average the $N-f$ best Krum-scored clients.

    \item[D.~Coordinate-wise Median.]
    $\theta^{(r+1)}_j = \operatorname{median}_i \theta_{i,j}^{(r)}$.

    \item[E.~Trimmed Mean.]
    Drop the top-$\beta$ and bottom-$\beta$ values per coordinate and
    average the rest. We use $\beta = 0.2$.

    \item[F.~Bulyan.]
    A Multi-Krum pre-filter ($N-2f$ survivors) followed by coordinate-wise
    trimmed mean.

    \item[G.~Proof-of-Contribution (PoC, ours).]
    Top-$K$ client selection ranked by
    \[
      \text{PoC}(i) = \underbrace{\mathrm{EMA}_{\alpha=0.7}\!\big(\text{acc}_{i,r}\big)}_{\text{behavioural recency}}
                       \times
                       \underbrace{\frac{|\mathcal{H}_i|}{r}}_{\text{participation}}
    \]
    computed from on-chain history $\mathcal{H}_i$. Weighted FedAvg over
    the top~$K=7$ selected clients, clamped to $(10^{-6}, 1-10^{-6})$.
    PoC also gates the synthetic-data approvals in Session~4 via the
    same score and the same $0.4$ threshold.
\end{description}

\begin{findingbox}[Why PoC is not Krum-with-History]
Krum defends by per-round geometry. PoC defends by \emph{behavioural
history}, which is strictly more information. Under a Gaussian attack,
geometry suffices (Krum and PoC both reject). Under a sleeper attack,
geometry \emph{fails} because the attacker looks geometrically honest for
the first 14 rounds; only behavioural EMA + participation can detect the
post-round-15 accuracy collapse the attack induces in the malicious
clients' own local validation. Section~\ref{sec:session3} shows this
numerically.
\end{findingbox}

\section{Generative Substrate: 1D UNet Class-Conditional Diffusion}
\label{sec:diffusion}

The latent diffusion generator is unchanged from the original Phase~3.1
document but is reproduced here because its outputs are central to
Session~4.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Diffusion Model Configuration}
\label{tab:diff}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Component} & \textbf{Configuration} \\
\midrule
Base model         & HuggingFace \texttt{UNet1DModel} \\
Sample size        & 384 (padded from 360, divisibility by $2^4$) \\
Input channels     & 2 (ECG + class-conditioning channel) \\
Output channels    & 1 (predicted noise) \\
Down blocks        & Down, Down, AttnDown, AttnDown \\
Up blocks          & AttnUp, AttnUp, Up, Up \\
Channel widths     & [32, 64, 128, 256] \\
Noise schedule     & DDPM, 1000 training timesteps \\
Pre-training       & 30 epochs on pooled honest-client data (once, pre-FL) \\
Inference steps    & 30 (wall-clock cut, no quality loss observed) \\
Conditioning       & Broadcast $c/4$ on a full-length channel, concat to noisy ECG \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Pre-training is performed once, on pooled ECG windows from the eight
honest clients, \textit{before} round~1. No test-set data leaks into the
generator. After round~1, the generator weights are frozen for the whole
FL schedule; only inference is invoked during rounds.

\textbf{Attack relevance:} because the generator is class-conditional via
channel concatenation, a malicious client at \textit{generation time} can
request synthetic APB samples while passing a flipped label, causing the
generator to denoise into a PVC-like waveform that is then stamped as APB
in the local DataLoader. This is the precise failure that the ledger's
immutable receipt chain (Section~\ref{sec:session4}) is designed to detect
and that Multi-Krum + Blind-LDM cannot prevent.

\subsection{Interpretation: The Dilution Effect}

\begin{findingbox}[Why FedAvg ``wins'' macro-F1 here --- and why this strengthens our narrative]
With only 2/10 attackers (20\%) and a Dirichlet-$0.5$ partition, honest
gradients are themselves highly diverse. FedAvg's coordinate-wise average
\textit{dilutes} the flipped-label gradients against a larger and more
diverse honest pool --- a passive defense mechanism. Simultaneously,
distance-based defenses (Krum, MultiKrum) punish themselves by
occasionally dropping \textit{large honest non-IID clients} whose high
Krum distances are caused by data heterogeneity, not maliciousness.

PoC's EMA-of-history score avoids this pathology: it tracks behavioural
accuracy, which is invariant to per-round geometric outliers. Hence PoC
matches FedAvg on macro-F1 ($0.9586$ vs $0.9613$, a $0.003$ gap) while
still being the second-best APB-F1 defense here ($0.8418$ vs
$0.8551$).

\textbf{The clinically significant observation is not the macro-F1 ranking
--- it is that Krum's APB-F1 collapses to $0.6456$.} Multi-Krum is also
suspect here: its APB-F1 is $0.6929$, worse than Median / Bulyan /
TrimmedMean. Distance-based filtering is hurting minority classes on
non-IID silos.
\end{findingbox}

\begin{warningbox}[The FedAvg Mirage]
FedAvg's $0.9613$ macro-F1 in Session 2 is consistent with the wider
FL literature on low-ratio attackers (cf.\ Multi-Krum paper, Bulyan
paper) but should not be read as a license to deploy FedAvg clinically.
The same algorithm collapsed to $0.1711$ in Session 1 under Gaussian, and
will show partial collapse under the sleeper attack of Session 3
(APB-F1 = $0.7614$). A safe FL stack must be robust \textit{across} attack
models, not tuned to any single one.
\end{warningbox}
